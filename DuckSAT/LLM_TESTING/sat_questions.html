Question 1:

We tend to comfort ourselves with tidy histories of innovation: a lone inventor has an idea, perfects a device, and ushers in a new era. But who gets remembered as the inventor often owes as much to storytelling and networks as to the invention itself. In 1907, Eliza Freund, a mechanical engineer working in a cramped workshop above a bakery, assembled a compact water filtration unit meant for tenement buildings. Her design, modest and sturdy, solved problems of cost and maintenance that had deterred landlords from installing filters. She published her schematics in a municipal engineering journal and demonstrated the unit at neighborhood meetings, where parents took home handwritten pamphlets. Two years later, a well-funded manufacturer released a polished, chrome-laden filtration system that employed Freund’s core principles—rapid sand filtration and modular cleaning—yet tucked them inside a gleaming casing. The company hired illustrators, secured endorsements from city physicians, and distributed free samples to schools. Within a decade, museum displays featured the manufacturer’s model behind glass, and textbook timelines paired its debut with “the dawn of urban water safety.” Freund’s unit, which had quietly reduced illness in crowded buildings, receded into footnotes. It is tempting to interpret this erasure as intentional theft, but the explanation is usually less conspiratorial and more systemic. The artifacts we elevate are the ones easiest to narrate and display, with names attached, dates convenient, and images that photograph well. Textbooks require a short arc; museum placards are brief. Engineers like Freund who publish in local journals, adapt designs iteratively, and sell to small clients create stories with frayed edges. Their work feeds progress, yet resists the neat plotlines we learn to memorize. If we want to honor innovation honestly, we must widen the aperture: recognize that progress tracks with story as much as with invention, and remember that credit is a fragile thing, easily guided by the hands that arrange the exhibits.

Explanation:

The passage argues that public recognition of innovation is influenced not solely by technical achievement but by the narratives, networks, and presentation that make certain inventions memorable and visible. The example of Freund’s filtration unit versus the manufacturer’s polished model illustrates how marketing, endorsements, and textbook-friendly stories create lasting credit. Therefore, the best statement of the claim emphasizes the role of storytelling and networks in determining who is remembered. Choice A contradicts the passage’s argument by implying technical excellence inevitably prevails. Choice C reduces the issue to patents, which the passage never presents as the central factor. Choice D imputes motives (lack of ambition) that are neither stated nor supported. Choice B captures the passage’s thesis.

A) Technical excellence inevitably prevails over weak marketing in determining historical credit. B) Public recognition of innovation depends on narrative and networks as much as on invention itself. MathInequalities

Question 16:

How many integer values of x satisfy both inequalities |x − 4| + |x + 1| ≤ 9 and (x − 5)/(x + 2) ≥ 0?

Explanation:

First, solve |x − 4| + |x + 1| ≤ 9 by breaking into cases around x = −1 and x = 4. - For x ≤ −1: |x − 4| = 4 − x and |x + 1| = −x − 1, so the sum is (4 − x) + (−x − 1) = 3 − 2x ≤ 9 ⇒ −2x ≤ 6 ⇒ x ≥ −3. Combined with x ≤ −1 gives −3 ≤ x ≤ −1. - For −1 ≤ x ≤ 4: |x − 4| = 4 − x and |x + 1| = x + 1, so the sum is 5, which is ≤ 9 for all x in this interval. - For x ≥ 4: |x − 4| = x − 4 and |x + 1| = x + 1, so the sum is 2x − 3 ≤ 9 ⇒ 2x ≤ 12 ⇒ x ≤ 6. Combined with x ≥ 4 gives 4 ≤ x ≤ 6. Combining all cases, the solution to the first inequality is −3 ≤ x ≤ 6. Next, solve (x − 5)/(x + 2) ≥ 0. The expression is undefined at x = −2 and equals zero at x = 5. Use sign analysis: - For x < −2: numerator (x − 5) < 0, denominator (x + 2) < 0 ⇒ ratio > 0 (included). - For −2 < x < 5: numerator < 0, denominator > 0 ⇒ ratio < 0 (excluded). - For x ≥ 5: numerator ≥ 0, denominator > 0 ⇒ ratio ≥ 0 (included). Note x = −2 is excluded (undefined), x = 5 is included (equals 0). Thus, the solution to the second inequality is x < −2 or x ≥ 5. Intersecting with −3 ≤ x ≤ 6 gives the set (−3 ≤ x < −2) ∪ (5 ≤ x ≤ 6). The integer solutions are x = −3, 5, and 6, for a total of 3 integers.

Answers:

3

Question 17:

Consider the system of equations below, where t is a real parameter: (t − 2)x + 4y = 8 3x + (t + 1)y = 5 a) For which value(s) of t does the system have no solution? b) For which value(s) of t does the system have infinitely many solutions? c) For t = 1, find the solution (x, y).

Explanation:

- A 2 × 2 linear system has a unique solution when the determinant of its coefficient matrix is nonzero. If the determinant is zero, the lines are either parallel distinct (no solution) or the same line (infinitely many solutions). - The coefficient matrix is: [ t − 2 4 ] [ 3 t + 1 ] Its determinant is Δ = (t − 2)(t + 1) − (4)(3) = t^2 − t − 14. - If Δ ≠ 0, the system has a unique solution. If Δ = 0, we must check proportionality of the entire equations (including constants) to distinguish between no solution and infinitely many solutions. a) Set Δ = 0: t^2 − t − 14 = 0 Using the quadratic formula, t = (1 ± √57)/2. At these t values, the coefficient rows are proportional (the lines have the same slope). To have infinitely many solutions, the constants must scale by the same factor as the coefficients; otherwise, the lines are parallel distinct with no solution. Let λ be the proportionality factor from the first equation to the second. From coefficients, λ = 3/(t − 2) = (t + 1)/4. For the constants to match, we would also need 5 = λ · 8, i.e., λ = 5/8. Solving 3/(t − 2) = 5/8 gives t = 34/5, and (t + 1)/4 = 5/8 gives t = 3/2. These do not match, so there is no t for which the entire equations are proportional. Therefore, when Δ = 0 (t = (1 ± √57)/2), the system has no solution. b) As shown above, there is no t that makes the entire second equation a constant multiple of the first (including the constants). Question 19:

Let P(x) be a polynomial. When P(x) is divided by x − 1, the remainder is 5; when divided by x − 2, the remainder is 7; and when divided by x − 3, the remainder is 9. What is the remainder when P(x) is divided by (x − 1)(x − 2)(x − 3)? A) x + 5 B) 2x + 3 C) x^2 − x + 4 D) 3x − 1

Explanation:

When a polynomial P(x) is divided by (x − 1)(x − 2)(x − 3), the remainder R(x) must be a polynomial of degree less than 3, so let R(x) = ax^2 + bx + c. Because (x − 1), (x − 2), and (x − 3) are factors of the divisor, we have R(1) = P(1), R(2) = P(2), and R(3) = P(3). The given remainders tell us: - R(1) = 5 → a + b + c = 5 - R(2) = 7 → 4a + 2b + c = 7 - R(3) = 9 → 9a + 3b + c = 9 Subtract the first equation from the second: 3a + b = 2. Subtract the second from the third: 5a + b = 2. Subtract these two new equations: (5a + b) − (3a + b) = 2 − 2 → 2a = 0 → a = 0. Then 3a + b = 2 → b = 2. Finally, a + b + c = 5 → 0 + 2 + c = 5 → c = 3. Therefore, R(x) = 2x + 3.

B) 2x + 3

MathPercentages

Question 23:

A store sells two products, A and B. Last year, the revenue from A and B was in the ratio 3:2. This year, the price of A increased by 25% and the price of B decreased by 10%. The quantities sold remained the same as last year. To keep the total revenue equal to last year’s total, the manager decides to apply a single percentage discount to both products’ new prices. What discount, to the nearest tenth of a percent, should the manager apply? A) 9.1% B) 9.9% C) 10.0% D) 11.0%

Explanation:

Let last year’s total revenue be R. Since A and B contributed in the ratio 3:2, A contributed (3/5)R and B contributed (2/5)R. After the price changes: - A’s revenue scales by 1.25, so A contributes 1.25 × (3/5)R = 0.75R. - B’s revenue scales by 0.90, so B contributes 0.90 × (2/5)R = 0.36R. Total revenue after the price changes is 0.75R + 0.36R = 1.11R, which is an 11% increase over last year. Applying a single discount of d% multiplies the new total by (1 − d). We want the final revenue to equal last year’s, so: 1.11 × (1 − d) = 1 1 − d = 1 / 1.11 = 100/111 ≈ 0.9009009 d ≈ 1 − 0.9009009 ≈ 0.099099 ≈ 9.9%

Answers:

B) 9.9%

MathStatistics & Probability

Question 24:

A bag is chosen at random with a fair coin. Bag A contains 3 red and 1 blue marble. Bag B contains 2 red and 2 blue marbles. From the chosen bag, two marbles are drawn without replacement. You are told that at least one of the marbles drawn is red. What is the probability that both marbles drawn are red? A) 1/3 B) 4/11 C) 1/2 D) 5/12 E) 2/5

Explanation:

Let R be the event that both marbles are red, and L be the event that at least one marble is red. Compute probabilities within each bag: - Bag A: P(R | A) = (3/4)(2/3) = 1/2. P(L | A) = 1 because you cannot draw two blues (there is only one blue). - Bag B: P(R | B) = (2/4)(1/3) = 1/6. P(L | B) = 1 − P(two blues) = 1 − (2/4)(1/3) = 1 − 1/6 = 5/6. Since the coin is fair, P(A) = P(B) = 1/2. Use the law of total probability: - P(R) = (1/2)(1/2) + (1/2)(1/6) = 1/4 + 1/12 = 1/3. - P(L) = (1/2)(1) + (1/2)(5/6) = 1/2 + 5/12 = 11/12. We want P(R | L) = P(R ∩ L) / P(L). Because R implies L, P(R ∩ L) = P(R). So: P(R | L) = (1/3) / (11/12) = (1/3) × (12/11) = 4/11.

Answers:

B) 4/11
